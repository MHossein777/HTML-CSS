Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II - American fifth-generation single-seat, single-engine multi-role fighter built by Lockheed Martin as part of the Joint Strike Fighter project , which was aimed at building a machine that would meet the requirements of all types of American armed forces . The prototype version of the aircraft was known as the X-35 JSF, but on July 7, 2006, the machine was officially renamed Lightning II . The F-35 Lightning II is capable of close support missions, bombing and fighter air combat missions. The aircraft project is jointly financed by the governments of the United States, Great Britain and other countries interested in purchasing the aircraft. The F-35 Lightning II was built under the direction of Lockheed Martin in collaboration with BAE Systems and Northrop Grumman . The JSF program was initiated to build a machine capable of replacing many types of aircraft, which is expected to reduce the costs of their production and maintenance. Ultimately, three versions of the F-35 are to be created, in which 80% of the structural elements will be identical, and the remaining 20% ​​will be dedicated systems, adapting the machine to perform specific tasks or activities in specific conditions.

History

Genesis Advanced Strike Technology With the end of the Cold War in the early 1990s, US military spending was reduced. After the collapse of the Soviet Union , savings began to be sought, and many arms programs were canceled or reduced. Launched in 1991, the Multi Role Fighter (MRF) program to build the successor to the General Dynamics F-16 Fighting Falcon aircraft used by the United States Air Force ended in 1993. A similar fate befell the AX programs, which was to emerge a joint attack aircraft for the Air Force and the United States Navy - the future successor of the Grumman A-6 Intruder machines , General Dynamics F-111 ,McDonnell Douglas F-15E Strike Eagle and Lockheed F-117 Nighthawk - and A / FX, which was also aimed at selecting a multipurpose fighter aircraft for the US Air Force and the Navy. The tendency for new constructions to meet the requirements of all three types of armed forces having their own air fleets (navy, US Air Force and United States Marine Corps ) appeared already in the early 1950s, when the North American F -86 Saberadapted to the role of an on-board machine. Airplanes designed in accordance with the requirements of all potential users could significantly contribute to reducing the costs of their construction. Unfortunately, often mutually exclusive requirements meant that there were practically no combat machines used by all types of armed forces. The exception is the McDonnell Douglas F-4 Phantom II , although it was originally commissioned by the Navy. On February 23, 1993, the Department of Defense began the so-called Bottom-Up Review (BUR), an analysis of the adaptation of the armed forces to operations according to the new defense doctrine in force in the political and military situation changed after the collapse of the Soviet Union. In September 1993, the conclusions of the BUR analysis were announced. One of them was the need for maximum unification of the equipment of the armed forces (including aviation). This solution was to reduce the cost of developing and building new structures and reducing the unit price. On the basis of the BUR analysis in September 1993, the MRF and A / FX programs were canceled, and in their place the Joint Advanced Strike Technology (JAST - Joint Advanced Strike Technology) program was established [4]. The JAST program formally began on January 27, 1994. Its goal was not so much to develop a ready-made aircraft for the US Air Force, US Navy and US Marine Corps, new weapons and avionics, but to test in practice the technology and design solutions for the new machine, which was planned to fly in around 1997, and to enter service in the final version, resulting from the work of the JAST program, for 2010. An equally important goal was to reduce the costs of developing and implementing new technologies into production. As a result, JAST was to end with the construction of the CTOL machine, which would complement the F-22 in the Air Force and replace the F-16 and A-10, as well as STOVL machines.for the US Marine Corps and Royal Navy, as successors to the Harriers and the F / A-18A-D, and the aircraft with conventional take-off and landing characteristics for US Navy aircraft carriers that would replace the F / A-18E / F. On September 2, 1994, the Department of Defense announced a call for Request for Proposal . On December 22 of the same year, the list of twenty-four companies with which initial contacts were signed as part of the phase known as Concept Definition and Design Research [4] [5] was announced. The contractors included Boeing Defense and Space Group, McDonnell Douglas Aerospace, Northrop Grumman and Lockheed Corporation, among others. In total, contracts were awarded for the order of $ 140 billion. The main differences between the designs concerned the choice of drive configuration for the STVOL version. Boeing proposed the most classic solution originally known as the Configuration AVX-70, very similar to Harrier's design: an engine with additional moving nozzles that deflect to change the thrust vector. Northrop Grumman proposed a solution known from the Soviet Yak -141 and Yak-36 planes: at the end of the marching engine there is a movable nozzle tilted downwards and an additional vertical take-off and landing supporting engine, placed vertically behind the pilot's cabin. The most innovative solution was proposed by Lockheed and McDonnell. Lockheed's design known as Configuration 100 proposed placing a pilot shaft fan directly behind the cockpit that supplies power from the main engine. A similar solution was adopted by McDonnell in his proposal known as Configuration 1, but in his design the turbine located behind the cabin was to be powered by gases discharged from the main engine.

Affordable Lightweight Fighter In 1983, DARPA (Agencja Advanced Defense Research Projects, then known as Advanced Research Projects Agency) started the Advanced Short Take-Off / Vertical Landing (ASTOVL) program. The program aimed to develop the technology needed to build a supersonic aircraft capable of replacing the AV-8B used by the United States Marine Corps and Harriery II of the Royal Air Force . In 1986, an agreement was signed to start cooperation between the Royal Aircraft Establishment (now Defense Evaluation and Research Agency) and DARPA [4] . In 1987, the program was handed over to Lockheed, which in its branch, unofficially known as Skunk Works, was developing with the help of NASA the construction of a supersonic fighter plane with STOVL properties . Lockheed proposed the creation of a joint combat platform with STOVL properties for the US Marine Corps, US Navy and US Air Force as part of the STOVL Strike Fighter (SSF) program. In addition to the features of shortened take-off and landing, the new plane would also have stealth properties. In 1993, the SSF program changed its name to become the Common Affordable Lightweight Fighter (CALF) program. The new program was designed to create a common platform for the Royal Navy and the US Marine Corps and a light fighter for the US Air Force. In 1994, the name of the program was changed again to Affordable Lightweight Fighter (ALF) [4] .

Joint Strike Fighter (JSF) X-35A during testing at Edwards Base In October 1995, the American Congress decided to merge the JAST and CALF programs. In June 1996, the combined program was renamed the Joint Strike Fighter [4] . In March 1996, the final requirements to be met by the new aircraft were set out with the announcement of another Request for Proposal [4] . At the beginning of the program, the demand for a new plane was estimated at 3002 units. 1763 of which were intended for the US Air Force to replace the A-10 and F-16. Another 480 intended for the US Navy would replace the F / A-18C / D machines. 609 aircraft would be purchased by the Marines, withdrawing their AV-8B and F / A-18. 150 machines would enter service with the Royal Navy withdrawing its Harriers [6]. The entire program has been divided into two preliminary phases. The first of them, known as the Concept Demonstration Program (CDP), was to end with the construction of a flying demonstrator used in the project and the future serial aircraft of technologies and design solutions. The second phase, known as the Preferred Weapon System Concept (PWSC - the preferred configuration of the combat version), was aimed at presenting the configuration of the on-board equipment used in the design, including the navigation and targeting system. On November 16, 1996, the Department of Defense announced that two companies, Boeing and Lockheed Martin, had been selected for the CDP phase [6] [7] . On May 28, 2002, an agreement on joining the research and development phase ( system design and development- SDD) of the JSF program was signed by Denmark with the US government, and on June 5 by the Netherlands [8] .

JSF partners The main partners of the Joint Strike Fighter program , which accounted for the majority of the design R&D expenditure, are the United States and the United Kingdom. In addition, by 2006, eight more countries had decided to jointly finance the project and would likely purchase a new machine in the future. The total cost of the development of the structure was estimated at over USD 55 billion , of which over 90% are American funds. Its cost increased from $ 34 billion in 2001, $ 40 billion in 2004 [15] , $ 44 billion in 2008 [16] to $ 55.13 billion in 2015 RDT & E (research, development, testing and evaluation), including 12 billion for development the F135 engine [17] .

F34A

The smallest and lightest version of Lightning intended for the USAF, where it is to replace the F-16 Fighting Falcon and A-10 Thunderbolty II. The only version equipped with a 25mm fixed cannon , more powerful than those used so far by the US Air Force since the introduction of the 20mm Vulcan cannons on the F-4E Phantom . On February 25, 2011, the first serial F-35A, designated AF-6, intended for the US Air Force, took off from the Naval Air Station Fort Worth Joint Reserve Base airport [50]. Lockheed test pilot Bill Gigiotti was at the controls of the plane that took off at 3:05 local time, after an hour's flight, the plane landed safely at 4:06 pm. The AF-6 was the seventeenth copy produced since 2006. The aircraft had the markings of the 58th Fighter Squadron from the 33rd Wing ( 33d Fighter Wing ) stationed at Eglin Air Force Base [51] . Norway equipped its planes with a braking parachute , not provided for in the project , for the use of icy belts [52] . Integration and tests of the parachute began in 2017 [53] , and Norwegian aircraft from the 8th LRIP series will be equipped with it [54] . Dutch planes are also to receive parachutes [55] . Canada, in addition to the parachute, is interested in equipping its F-35A (CF-35) with an in-flight refueling probe, an installation used on its CF-18 and available only on the F-35B / C [56]. Lockheed, anticipating that many potential foreign customers would be interested in the F-35A with the possibility of refueling with a flexible hose, left space in the hull for the integration of this installation (duplicating the fuel receiver used in the F-35A in accordance with the USAF requirements)

F-35B

The STOVL short take-off and vertical landing aircraft, which is to replace the previously used Harriers, becomes the first serial supersonic machine with STOVL capabilities. The Marines plan to replace both Harriers and F / A-18 Hornet fighters with a machine similar to the F-35A, which will have larger fuel tanks at the expense of vertical flight systems. As in the Harriers, the firearms will be carried in a centrally mounted overhead bunker. So far, vertical flight is the most dangerous phase of flight and the development of appropriate safety systems and reliable mechanisms has a significant impact on the development of the machine. Instead of Harry's rotating main engine nozzles and additional maneuvering nozzles, the F-35B uses an innovative Lockheed Martin patented and Rolls-Royce shaft-driven fan technology. This solution consists in installing in the middle of the fuselage a horizontally located two-rotor counter-rotating fan driven by a clutch and gearfrom the main engine of the machine. The fan is responsible for the vertical movement of the machine, while the discharge of exhaust gases from the main nozzle, through special bypasses, to the controlled nozzles on the wings and sides of the fuselage, causes the plane to rotate. As a result, the F-35B engine drives two turbines, a carrier and a propulsion turbine, which perform the same tasks as a large Harrier turbine, but also allow flight at supersonic speeds. The presence of the fan results in a 30% lower fuel reserve, 35 cm shorter armament chambers, and the cockpit fairing is smaller compared to other versions. Two prototype machines were built for flight tests: the X-35A, which was later rebuilt to the X-35B version, and the X-35C equipped with larger wings. The X-35B first took off vertically on June 24, 2001. Simon Hargeaves, BAE Systems test pilot was at the controls of the machine. The machine rose to a height of 7.5 m and remained hovering for about 35 seconds and then landed vertically [58] .

F-35C

F-35A version for the Israeli Air Force with Israeli weapons and additional systems. It was given the local name of Adir [60] . The first batch of 19-20 units to be delivered from 2016 is to differ from USAF planes only with the installation of the Israeli communication and data exchange system (C4I). Access to the source code of the fighter software, necessary for integration or modernization, which is also sought by Turkey and the United Kingdom, remains unclear [61] . The planes are to carry, inter alia, the guided bombs of the Rafael Spike series [62] . Israel operates the F-16I variant, which uses avionics elements produced by Israeli companies IAI , Elbit (Elop), Rafael , Elisra, Elta and Astronautics, i.e. HUD, computer, radio, data link, electronic warfare system, self-defense systems or self -alien identification system [63] . The first flight of the first F-35I took place on July 25, 2016 in Fort Worth [64] . By the end of 2016, Israel had ordered a total of fifty Adirs in three tranches [65] In May 2018, the use of the F-35I for the first time in combat was announced [66] . The air force commander, General Amikam Norkin, specified that the F-35s took part in two actions. In addition, he showed a photo of the Israeli F-35 that was flying over Beirut.
